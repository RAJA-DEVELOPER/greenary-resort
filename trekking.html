Before Your Trek

How to Prepare

4 Weeks Out
Physical Conditioning
Begin daily 30-minute walks, gradually increasing distance and elevation. Practice with a loaded pack 2 weeks before departure.
2 Weeks Out
Gear Preparation
Break in your boots. Confirm kit list with your guide. Arrange travel insurance with adventure activity coverage.
1 Week Out
Altitude Acclimatisation
For high-altitude routes: arrive 2-3 days early. Stay hydrated. Avoid alcohol. Rest and gentle walks at base elevation.
Arrival Day
Expert Briefing
Meet your guide team, review the route, check gear, and enjoy a hearty base camp dinner before an early start.
Essential Gear
Waterproof Boots

Ankle support, waterproof membrane, broken in for comfort.

30L Daypack

Lightweight, with rain cover, hip belt and chest strap.

Layered Clothing

Moisture-wicking base layer, fleece mid-layer, windproof outer.

2L Hydration System

Reservoir or water bottles. Purification tablets for longer routes.

Expert Advice

Trekker's Wisdom

Start at Dawn

Begin before 6am. The forest is most alive at dawn - and you'll avoid afternoon heat and possible afternoon rain at altitude.

Hydrate Ahead

Drink before you feel thirsty. At altitude, dehydration accelerates. Carry at least 2L per person for every 6 hours of trekking.

Pause and Observe

The best moments happen when you stop. Allow silence. Your guide knows when to pause - trust them, and let the forest reveal itself.

Medical Safety

Altitude Protocol

For our summit treks reaching above 2,500m, acclimatisation is critical. Our guides monitor blood oxygen levels twice daily and employ a strict 'climb high, sleep low' philosophy on all multi-day traverses to ensure optimal physiological adaptation.

Trail Sightings

Life on the Path

Avian Encounters

High ridge trails offer unique opportunities to spot majestic raptors riding the thermals, as well as colourful endemic species in the cloud forests.

Alpine Botanicals

Above 2,000m, the landscape transforms. Rare orchids, medicinal herbs, and resilient alpine shrubs decorate the rocky outcrops.

Tracking Mammals

Our guides will teach you to read the trail-identifying leopard pugmarks, deer scat, and bear scratchings on ancient tree trunks.

Zero Compromise

Navigation & Safety Protocol

While you experience absolute wilderness, our invisible safety net is always active. Every guide carries a satellite communicator, comprehensive medical kit, and GPS tracking device. Our base camp operations team monitors all deep-wilderness expeditions in real-time.
